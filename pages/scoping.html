Chapter 6

Scoping Complexity and Asset Categorization

Home › Scoping

A common point of failure in C3PAO assessments is the misidentification of the assessment boundary. Scoping is not merely about identifying where the CUI is stored — it involves a complex analysis of every asset that can influence the security of that information.

The Five Asset Classes

Asset ClassDescriptionAssessment Scope
CUI AssetsSystems that process, store, or transmit CUIFull — all 110 CMMC requirements
Security Protection AssetsTools and services that protect the environment (firewalls, SIEMs, managed security)Full assessment
Contractor Risk Managed Assets (CRMA)Systems that can, but are not intended to, process CUILimited — must be documented in SSP
Specialized AssetsIoT devices, operational technology (OT), test equipmentIn scope but assessed against limited criteria
Out-of-Scope AssetsSystems physically and logically isolated from CUI environmentNot assessed

The C3PAO's Role in Scoping

The C3PAO's role during the scoping call is to challenge the OSC's boundary definitions. If an organization fails to account for a remote work access point or a third-party cloud connection, the C3PAO will flag this as a risk to the assessment's integrity.

CUI Enclave Strategy: Effective scoping often leads to the creation of a "CUI Enclave" — a segregated part of the network that limits the assessment footprint and, consequently, reduces the C3PAO's fees and the OSC's implementation burden.

Common Scoping Mistakes

  • Overlooking remote access: VPN endpoints, remote desktop solutions, and BYOD policies can expand the boundary significantly
  • Ignoring cloud connections: SaaS tools and cloud storage that touch CUI must be included
  • Misclassifying security tools: Firewalls, SIEM platforms, and MDR services are Security Protection Assets and fully in scope
  • Forgetting shared infrastructure: Printers, scanners, and shared drives that may incidentally contact CUI
